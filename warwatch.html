1945–48
Context
WW2 ends — Middle East redrawn by colonial powers
After World War 2, Britain and France carved up the Middle East into new countries, often ignoring who actually lived there. The US emerged as the dominant Western power, determined to keep the region's oil flowing to the West.
Iran had oil. Israel was about to be created. The seeds of every conflict since were planted in these few years by decisions made in London, Paris and Washington.
1953
Turning Point
CIA overthrows Iran's elected leader — installs the Shah
Iran's popular prime minister Mohammad Mosaddegh tried to nationalise Iranian oil so Iranians could benefit from it. Britain and America couldn't allow that. The CIA organised a coup, removed him, and put a pro-Western king — the Shah — back in power.
Iranians never forgot this. The 1953 coup is the single most important reason Iran distrusts the US today. Every Iranian government since has referenced it. It is the original wound.
1967–73
Context
Israel defeats Arab armies — occupies Palestinian territories
In the 1967 Six-Day War, Israel defeated Egypt, Jordan and Syria in just six days, capturing the West Bank, Gaza, Sinai and Golan Heights. Six years later, Egypt and Syria tried to take back their land in the 1973 Yom Kippur War — they nearly succeeded before US support turned the tide for Israel.
These wars established Israel as the dominant military power in the region, backed firmly by the US. For Arab and Muslim countries — including Iran — this partnership became the defining grievance in the region.
1979
Revolution
Iran's Islamic Revolution — America loses its ally
The Shah's brutal dictatorship finally collapsed. Ayatollah Khomeini led a revolution that threw out the Shah and replaced him with an Islamic government that saw America as the Great Satan. Iranian students stormed the US Embassy and held 52 Americans hostage for 444 days.
Overnight, the US lost its most important ally in the region. Iran went from friend to enemy. The nuclear programme that would consume the next 40 years began in secret around this time — though Iran claims it was always peaceful.
1980–88
War
US backs Saddam Hussein in war against Iran
Iraq invaded Iran in 1980. Millions died over eight years in one of the bloodiest wars since WW2. The US quietly backed Iraq — providing Saddam Hussein with intelligence, weapons and money — because America wanted Iran weakened.
Iran emerged exhausted but unbroken. The experience hardened the Islamic Republic's belief that it needed its own deterrence — its own weapons — because the world would always side against Iran when convenient.
1990s–2000s
Nuclear
Iran secretly builds nuclear programme
While publicly denying it, Iran built uranium enrichment facilities at Natanz and Fordow. The IAEA began raising alarms. Iran said it was for electricity. The US and Israel were convinced it was for a bomb.
By the early 2000s, Iran was a few years away from being able to build a nuclear weapon if it chose to. Israel began calling it an existential threat. The US began drawing red lines. The countdown clock started.
2003
Iraq War
US invades Iraq — Iran's enemy is removed for free
America invaded Iraq and toppled Saddam Hussein — the same man America had backed against Iran 20 years earlier. With Saddam gone, Iran's biggest regional enemy disappeared overnight. Iran's influence flooded into Iraq almost immediately.
Iran also drew a sharp lesson from watching Saddam fall: leaders who give up weapons programmes get overthrown. Libya's Gaddafi gave up his nuclear programme in 2003 — and was killed in 2011. Iran decided it would never make the same mistake.
2006–12
Shadow War
Israel and Iran begin shadow war — assassinations, explosions
Iranian nuclear scientists began dying in mysterious accidents. Explosions hit Iranian facilities. Iran-linked groups attacked Israeli targets abroad. This undeclared war was run through intelligence agencies — Mossad, CIA, IRGC — without either side formally admitting it.
Israel repeatedly told US leaders it was preparing a military strike on Iran's nuclear sites. The US repeatedly said wait. The tension between Israeli urgency and American caution defined this entire era.
2015
Diplomacy
Iran nuclear deal signed — brief hope
After years of sanctions crippling Iran's economy, a historic deal was reached. Iran agreed to freeze and reduce its nuclear programme in exchange for sanctions relief. The US, UK, France, Germany, China and Russia all signed it. For a moment it looked like the conflict might be resolved through diplomacy.
It didn't last. Iran continued supporting Hezbollah and Hamas. Israel lobbied furiously against the deal. The regional proxy wars continued. And in 2018, Trump withdrew the US from the deal, calling it the worst deal ever made.
2018–24
Escalation
US leaves nuclear deal — Iran accelerates enrichment
After Trump withdrew from the nuclear deal, Iran responded by dramatically accelerating its uranium enrichment — enriching to 60% purity, well beyond what any civilian programme needs. Iran was now months, not years, from weapons-grade material.
Simultaneously, Iran's proxy network expanded across the region. Hezbollah in Lebanon, Houthis in Yemen, militias in Iraq and Syria — all armed, funded and directed by Iran. Israel was surrounded. The question was no longer if a war would happen, but when.
2024–25
Gaza
Gaza war — the fuse is lit
Hamas's October 7 2023 attack on Israel killed 1,200 Israelis. Israel's response killed over 40,000 Palestinians in Gaza. Hezbollah opened a second front from Lebanon. The Houthis in Yemen began attacking ships in the Red Sea. Iran was behind all three.
By early 2025, Israel had killed Hezbollah's leader Nasrallah, destroyed much of Hezbollah's military capacity, and was openly discussing striking Iran directly. The US was trying to hold Israel back. Both sides were running out of patience.
Feb 28, 2026
War Begins
Operation Epic Fury begins — the war no one stopped
On February 28 2026, Israel conducted a massive strike on Iranian territory, followed within hours by US air support under Operation Epic Fury. The stated goal was to permanently destroy Iran's nuclear programme before Iran crossed the final threshold.
The attack was the culmination of 80 years of history — the 1953 coup, the 1979 revolution, the nuclear deal collapse, the Gaza war, the proxy wars — all roads led here. The region's longest cold war finally went hot.

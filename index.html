US bombs Iran's Fordow nuclear facility — the biggest strike in conflict history
America sent warplanes and missiles to destroy the underground factory in Iran where they were building nuclear weapons. This is the first time the US has directly attacked Iranian soil.

The United States Air Force (USAF) launched a major operation using B-2 stealth bombers carrying GBU-57 bunker-buster bombs against the Fordow Fuel Enrichment Plant (FFEP) — Iran's most fortified nuclear site, buried 90 metres underground near Qom.

The Islamic Republic of Iran (IRI) had been enriching uranium to 84% purity — just one step below the 90% needed for a nuclear weapon. Intelligence from the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA), Israel's Mossad, and the UK's MI6 all concluded Iran was 2–3 weeks from completing its first bomb.

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) had been unable to inspect the site for 8 months after Iran expelled its inspectors. This was the final red line for the US-Israel alliance.

President Trump authorised the strike after Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu gave a 48-hour ultimatum: act now or Israel would strike alone, risking a wider uncontrolled regional war. The US Central Command (CENTCOM), based in Qatar, coordinated the operation.

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) immediately went on full war footing. The UN Security Council (UNSC) convened an emergency session within hours. Global oil prices jumped 18% in a single trading session.

Why did this happen now?
Iran was 2 weeks from a nuclear bomb. Israel issued an ultimatum — act now or we will. The US chose to strike first to prevent a larger uncontrolled war. This directly follows Iran expelling IAEA inspectors 8 months ago and enriching uranium to near-weapons grade.

Iran fires ballistic missiles at US bases in Iraq and Syria
After the US bombing, Iran fought back by launching large missiles at American military camps in Iraq and Syria. Most were intercepted by US missile defence systems. No casualties confirmed.

Within 4 hours of the US strikes, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched 22 ballistic missiles at two targets: Ain al-Assad Air Base in western Iraq and Al-Tanf Garrison in Syria.

The US Army Patriot (PAC-3) air defence systems and Israeli Arrow-3 interceptors shot down 19 of 22 missiles. Three landed inside base perimeters but in uninhabited zones — no US casualties were confirmed.

Iran's Supreme National Security Council (SNSC) called the strike only the "first wave" and threatened further escalation. The Strait of Hormuz was briefly blockaded by Iranian naval vessels before reopening under international pressure.

How is this connected to the past?
In January 2020, the US killed Iran's top general Soleimani at Baghdad airport. Iran retaliated then by firing missiles at the exact same Ain al-Assad base. History is repeating — same target, same playbook, six years later.

Kharg Island — Iran's oil lifeline — now on Israel's target list
Kharg Island is where Iran ships 90% of its oil to the world. Israel has placed it on its strike list, which could trigger a global oil price crisis felt as far as India.

Kharg Island in the Persian Gulf handles over 90% of Iran's oil exports — approximately 1.5 million barrels per day. This single location generates nearly all of Iran's foreign currency.

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) confirmed to the Knesset that Kharg Island is "actively on the target list" if Iran escalates further. Brent crude jumped from $74 to $97 per barrel in 48 hours.

India imports approximately 7% of its oil from Iran. The Indian Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas (MoPNG) has directed state refiners to urgently seek alternative supply from the UAE, Iraq, and the US Gulf Coast.

Why does this affect India and you?
If Kharg Island is bombed, global oil could exceed $130 per barrel. Petrol in India could surpass ₹120/litre. Food delivery, auto fares, flight tickets — everything transported by vehicle becomes more expensive.

Timeline — how it started
1948 to today
1948
1948
Israel is founded — and immediately attacked
After the Holocaust in World War II, Jewish people established the state of Israel in the Middle East. The very next day, five Arab countries attacked it. Israel survived. But the conflict over land and identity has never been fully resolved — it remains at the heart of everything happening today.
1979
1979 · Root cause
Iran's Islamic Revolution — the moment everything changed
Religious leader Ayatollah Khomeini overthrew Iran's pro-American government. The new leadership declared Israel an enemy and America "the Great Satan." Iran began funding Hamas and Hezbollah to fight Israel. This single event is the root cause of today's entire conflict — without 1979, none of this exists.
Root cause. Every event that follows grows from here.
2003
2003
World discovers Iran's secret nuclear programme
Iran had been secretly building uranium enrichment facilities for years. When the IAEA discovered this, the West imposed heavy economic sanctions. Iran refused to stop. The nuclear standoff that eventually led to today's strikes began here.
2015
2015 · Brief peace
JCPOA nuclear deal — a brief moment of peace
Under President Obama, the US and Iran signed the JCPOA — Iran agreed to slow its nuclear programme in exchange for sanctions relief. Iran's economy improved. But in 2018, Trump pulled the US out. Iran immediately restarted its programme. The collapse of this deal directly led to today's crisis.
Connected to today: had the US not left the deal in 2018, Iran would never have gotten this close to a bomb.
2020
Jan 2020 · Turning point
US kills Iran's top general Qasem Soleimani
The US killed Iran's most powerful military commander in a drone strike at Baghdad airport. Soleimani ran Iran's entire proxy army network across the Middle East. Iran retaliated by firing missiles at US bases in Iraq — including Ain al-Assad Air Base. Iran vowed long-term revenge.
Directly connected to today: Iran fired missiles at the exact same Ain al-Assad base again today. Same target, same playbook, six years later.
2023
Oct 2023 · The spark
Hamas attacks Israel — 1,200 killed
Hamas fighters broke through Israel's border and attacked villages, killing 1,200 people — the deadliest day for Jewish people since the Holocaust. Israel declared war on Gaza. Iran celebrated. The US deployed two aircraft carrier groups to the region. This event escalated the entire conflict to what we see today.
The spark that ignited today's fire. Every event from Oct 7 onwards flows directly from this day.

Why each event is connected
The chain of cause and effect
Iran's 1979 revolution created a government whose founding principle is opposition to Israel and the US
Before 1979, Iran and the US were allies. The revolution replaced Iran's pro-American government with religious leaders who viewed America and Israel as enemies — not as a changeable policy, but as a core founding principle of the state. Every single event in this conflict traces back to this single moment in history.
↓
Iran funded Hamas and Hezbollah to fight Israel without fighting directly
Iran knew it could not defeat Israel and the US in direct warfare. So it built proxy armies — groups in other countries that fight on Iran's behalf. Hamas in Gaza and Hezbollah in Lebanon became Iran's weapons against Israel. Decades of funding, missiles, and training built the threat that exists today.
↓
Hamas used that support to carry out the October 7, 2023 attack on Israel
The attack that killed 1,200 Israelis was made possible by decades of Iranian funding and weapons. Iran did not order the specific date — but Hamas could never have launched an operation of this scale without Iran's long-term support. This is the direct link between Iran and that day.
↓
As Israel dismantled Hamas in Gaza, Iran had to choose — act directly or lose all credibility
Iran's entire regional strategy depended on its proxy armies. As Israel destroyed Hamas, Iran faced a choice: intervene directly and risk war, or watch its proxy be eliminated and lose all influence. In April 2024, Iran chose to act — attacking Israel directly for the first time in its history.
↓
Iran accelerated its nuclear programme — seeing a bomb as the only true guarantee of survival
Iran studied countries like Iraq and Libya — both gave up their weapons programmes and were later attacked. Iran's conclusion: a nuclear weapon is the only protection that cannot be negotiated away or bombed away. So Iran pushed its enrichment programme to near-completion.
↓
Today — the result of 45 years of connected choices
A nuclear Iran changes the Middle East permanently. It emboldens every proxy group Iran funds. It forces Saudi Arabia and Turkey to pursue their own nuclear programmes. The US and Israel concluded that preventing this — even at the cost of a serious confrontation — was better than living with it.
